about this experience

the 8 is a biometric movement installation that asks a simple question: can you find your way to a fractal state? One participant at a time stands in front of the screen, picks up the stick, and moves freely while their DFA alpha 1 is measured in real time. The goal is presence. The music mines when your nervous system organises itself into that particular quality of aliveness that sits between order and chaos.

what is DFA alpha 1?

Imagine your heartbeat as a river. A random river (α ≈ 0.5) carries zero memory. Each ripple is independent of the last, producing pure noise with low structure. A frozen river (α ≈ 1.5) is over-correlated: every ripple simply copies the previous one, rigid and predictable. A fractal river (α ≈ 1.0) carries just the right amount of memory. Complex, self-similar across time scales, and endlessly adaptive. That is what a healthy, engaged nervous system looks like.

DFA alpha 1 (Detrended Fluctuation Analysis, short-range scaling exponent) captures this by analysing the pattern of fluctuations in a physiological signal across many time windows simultaneously. When those fluctuations scale like pink noise, the autonomic nervous system is in a self-organising, adaptive state. That is the zone. That is the glow.

how it's computed
  • The input signal can be RR intervals from a heart rate sensor (time between beats, in milliseconds) or acceleration magnitude from a motion sensor. Both reveal the same underlying fractal structure. In this installation, SomaSync samples acceleration at 250ms intervals, producing a continuous 4Hz data stream.
  • The signal is demeaned and integrated into a cumulative sum, which reveals the underlying structure of fluctuations across time.
  • The integrated signal is divided into windows of many different sizes. Here the analysis window is 128 samples. At 4Hz that equals a 32-second window of movement or heartbeat data. Shorter windows capture fast dynamics; longer windows capture slow ones.
  • Inside each window, a straight line is fit to remove local trends. The residual fluctuation around that line is measured and averaged across all segments of that window size.
  • This process repeats across all window sizes. Plotting log(window size) against log(average fluctuation) produces a straight line. The slope of that line is alpha.
  • A slope of 1.0 means the fluctuations scale the same way at every window size: the fractal signature. A new DFA value is produced every 2 seconds, incorporating 8 fresh samples per update.
what your score says about you
  • 0.75–1.0 (glow) adaptive zone. Parasympathetic dominance, autonomic resilience, flow state. Your system is organising complexity in real time.
  • 0.5–0.75 (tension) physiological load is rising. Common during moderate exercise or stress. The fractal structure grows less coherent.
  • below 0.5 (chaos) high load or fragmentation. Seen near exhaustion or in acute stress. The river is turbulent.
  • above 1.0 (stable/rigid) over-correlated. Often seen at deep rest or in states of reduced autonomic complexity. The river has slowed considerably.
DFA alpha 1 vs HRV: how they're different

Standard HRV metrics like RMSSD measure how much your heart rate varies. DFA alpha 1 measures how it varies: the fractal structure of that variation. You can have high HRV and low alpha (lots of variation with low structure), or modest HRV with alpha near 1.0 (small but exquisitely structured variation). During movement, standard HRV collapses at higher intensities and becomes hard to interpret. DFA alpha 1 remains meaningful throughout, which is why it works as a real-time signal during free movement. They are complementary: HRV tells you the amplitude; alpha tells you the architecture.

fun facts
  • DFA was originally invented to study DNA sequences before anyone thought to apply it to heartbeats. Peng et al. made that leap in 1995.
  • The fractal heart was identified by Ary Goldberger at Harvard, who noticed that healthy hearts look like coastlines: irregular at every scale, self-similar across all of them.
  • Pink noise (1/f noise, α = 1) shows up in music, brainwaves, river flows, forest fires, and financial markets. It is the signature of complex systems balanced at the edge of order and chaos.
  • DFA alpha 1 drops reliably below 0.75 at the aerobic threshold during exercise, making it a practical way to find your sustainable effort ceiling outside a lab setting.
  • During deep sleep, alpha drifts toward 1.5. The body becomes more rigid and less adaptive. Waking pulls it back toward 1.0.
  • The Dune sandwalk: walking on loose sand without sinking requires matching the terrain's natural rhythm. Too rigid, you punch through. Too random, you slide. Fractal movement finds the grain.
